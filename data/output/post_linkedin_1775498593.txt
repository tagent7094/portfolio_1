30 late-stage founders I talked to this year said 'AI-first.' Four had actually built something.

If you work at a late-stage startup where the founder keeps telling everyone to "use more AI" but isn't personally building tools, apps, or workflows with it, that company probably won't exist in five years.

Here's what people miss. It's not about the AI.

It's about what AI exposes.

A founder who delegates the future to a slide deck was already delegating the present. AI just made it visible. You can't ask your team to adopt a technology you haven't sat with. You can't tell people to change how they work when you won't change how you work.

I earned my first million myself. Not because I couldn't hire a VP of Sales. Because a founder who outsources conviction outsources the company.

Meanwhile, there are CEOs running "AI transformation initiatives" from conference rooms. Never built a workflow. Never broken an integration. Never sat with a customer and watched the tool fail in real time.

This isn't about management. It's about who's actually in the fight. The leaders who build, who sit in the mess, who use the thing before they sell it. Those are the ones shaping what comes next.

The rest are narrating someone else's war.

Someone right now is polishing a roadmap for a customer they haven't visited once this quarter. AI didn't make that roadmap irrelevant. It just made it obvious.
