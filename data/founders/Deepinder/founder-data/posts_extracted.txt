--- POST 1 ---
"We hit 107% of our gross pipeline goals, but we don’t know how."

That's what CMO of over a $5B company told me on our first call. And this is very different than hearing, we hit 70% of our targets, and we need to do better, tell me how can you help.

He said, "It’s always a nice thing when marketing is hitting close to 107% of its gross pipeline goals. You set these goals based on a ton of math and guesstimations, and sure enough, with three days left in the business days left in the quarter, you hit the goal. That’s seems unrealistic that, how did we get here? how do we repeat this?"

Every CMO will recognize this because they have all been in the QBR where the numbers are green, CEO is happy, CFO is willing to give more budget, but nobody in the room can explain which specific decisions made it green.

The danger isn't the quarter you can't explain. It's the quarter after.

Because the board sees 107% and assumes the machine works. So they raise targets. Now you need to replicate something you never understood in the first place, but at 120% scale.
- You set next quarter's goals based on results you can't attribute.
- You fund the channels that happened to be running when pipeline showed up.
- You starve the ones that weren't visible at the right moment.

Every quarter, the gap between your numbers and your understanding of those numbers gets wider.

What I respect about this particular CMO is what he did next. He didn't take the win and move on. He said, "I know what I don't know."

That's rare. Most leaders in his position do one of three things. They try to hack together dashboards internally. They assign some analyst to reverse-engineer attribution after the fact. Or they just choose to fly blind and hope the next quarter is green too.

The ones who actually solve it are the ones who treat the 107% quarter not as a success story but as a warning.

Because a number you can't explain is a number you can't defend. And the first time the board asks why you missed after four quarters of hitting, "we don't know how we did it before either" is not an answer that keeps you in the room.

--- POST 4 ---
A VP of Marketing I know got promoted in Q1, but she was put on a performance improvement plan in Q3.

What changed?

One quarter the attribution model credited her channels. The next quarter, it didn't. She didn't change but the attribution logic did.

B2B attribution models are not objective measurement systems. They're political documents. They tell whatever story the last person who configured them wanted to tell.

- First-touch models make demand gen look heroic.
- Last-touch models make SDR look like the entire engine.
- Multi-touch models spread credit so thin nobody looks good and nobody can be held accountable for anything.

The VP who felt like she was winning one quarter and drowning the next wasn't experiencing performance volatility. She was experiencing model volatility.

Her actual work didn't change, but the lens did she was being evaluated changed. So, until companies stop confusing the lens with reality, they'll keep promoting and firing the same people for the same invisible reasons.

--- POST 6 ---
The CMO has the shortest average tenure of any C-suite role. Most people think they know why. They don't.

We blame the talent, strategy, or alignment. But here's what actually happens.

Every time a CMO exits, they take two things with them. The demand generation thesis. And the brand narrative. The logic of why this market, this message, this channel mix.

What stays behind is the CRM configuration, the lead scoring model, the channel weights - all outputs of decisions nobody wrote down. Built during a busy quarter by someone who's now gone. Configured the way it is because that's what the last rev ops person knew how to do.

The next CMO inherits that system and spends the first six months trying to reverse-engineer it. Why is the MQL threshold set at that value? Why does this campaign attribute to first-touch and that one to last? Why is the LinkedIn budget four times the search budget?

Nobody knows. The person who decided is gone.

Most CMOs never fully figure it out. So they do the only thing they can. They add their own layer of decisions on top of what they inherited.

By the time they're actually being assessed on what they've built, they realize they've spent the entire tenure excavating someone else's thinking. They never got to lay their own foundation.

This is the flywheel nobody talks about.

CMOs don't keep failing because the role attracts bad talent. They keep failing because every 18 months the institutional memory of how marketing arrived at what it is gets wiped completely clean.

And the next person starts digging from the same spot, before they also leave.

--- POST 7 ---
Marketers have the job everyone has an opinion on.

Every CEO, CFO, and CRO has an opinion on marketing. Which makes sense, they’ve been on the receiving end of it their whole lives. But somehow that turns into thinking they’re qualified to judge it.

Nobody does this with finance or engineering. No one walks into a meeting and tells the CFO how to structure a balance sheet just because they’ve looked at one before.

But marketing gets that treatment every single day. The CMO is the only C-suite executive whose expertise is routinely overridden by people whose only qualification is being a consumer.

And over time, it changes how a CMO operates.

They stop bringing the raw version of what they’re seeing in the market. Instead, they bring the version they know will land well in the room. The channel that doesn't feel impressive in a board deck gets cut. The message that actually resonates with buyers but sounds too simple in a leadership meeting never ships.

On paper, it looks like the company just has different opinions about marketing. In reality, it’s losing the clearest signal it has about what customers actually think.

So when a CMO underperforms, companies often assume it’s a marketing problem.

What they actually have is a room full of consumers who confused familiarity with expertise, and a CMO who learned very quickly that telling them the truth was a faster path out the door than telling them what they wanted to hear.

--- POST 12 ---
The CMO seat at a B2B SaaS is the only C-suite role designed to fail.

The issue is rarely the capability of the person in the role. It comes from how the role is positioned inside the company.

Marketing becomes everyone’s side responsibility. Finance owns finance. Sales owns sales. But marketing is the only function where the budget holder does not fully control the levers that determine success.

Without a clear line between marketing activity and company priorities, a CMO’s authority never fully forms.

You can feel it in meetings. A senior executive pushes a different view and the energy changes. The CMO goes from leading the conversation to explaining why the plan makes sense.

The CMOs who hold real authority do one thing consistently. They anchor every conversation in the metric that keeps the company alive.

Revenue.

Every initiative, every campaign, every hire ties back to revenue impact. Conversations start there and end there. When marketing is clearly framed as a revenue engine, influence stops feeling borrowed. Until that connection is obvious, the CEO ends up acting as the real CMO. Control naturally flows to the person closest to the revenue number.

This dynamic has little to do with ego. It has everything to do with clarity. When marketing’s impact on revenue is obvious, trust grows. When it feels abstract, authority shrinks and someone else steps in.

MrBeast doesn't guarantee Salesforce wins the deal. He guarantees they make the list.

--- POST 57 ---
Inefficient handoffs and missing semantics once cost NASA $125M.

In 1998, the Mars Climate Orbiter was lost because of a unit mismatch. One team sent thruster data in pound-seconds. Another expected newton-seconds.

Both were “right” locally, but wrong globally. The boundary didn’t share meaning, so every course correction nudged the spacecraft slightly off. Over time, those small errors compounded. It entered the Martian atmosphere tens of kilometers too low and contact was lost on arrival day.

Every team had verified their part. None had validated the handoffs.

That’s the real lesson: It wasn’t a calculation error that doomed the Mars Orbiter. It was a handoff failure. GTM teams make the same mistake every quarter.

Marketing’s MQL, SDR’s Accepted, Sales’ Qualified - all are locally consistent, but semantically different. A lead becomes an opportunity without shared context.

These small drifts compound. And you end up missing your quarter.

Funnel definitions are your unit system in GTM funnel. Treat them as contracts, not suggestions. Preserve memory across handoffs. Make boundaries unambiguous.

The disaster never lives in the parts. It lives in the interfaces.
