The question I get from every new board member is the same: "What happens when the big players build this?"

They have been building it. Google has a product understanding layer. Amazon has A9 and Rufus. Every major platform is investing in product intelligence.

None of them solve the language gap for the brand. They solve it for the platform. Google wants to understand your product so Google can serve ads. Amazon wants to understand your product so Amazon can sell it.

Nobody except the brand has an incentive to make sure the brand's product content reflects the brand's customer's language. That is the gap we sit in. And it is a gap the big players will never close because it is not in their interest to close it.

Platform AI makes the platform smarter. Brand-side AI makes the brand smarter. Those are different businesses with different incentives.

Our customers understand this. That is why they build with us, not against us.

### B3

I am ten years into a company that does one thing: close the gap between how brands describe products and how consumers search for them.

Ten years. One problem. And the problem is bigger today than when we started.

Not because we failed to make progress. We have. Brands from Abercrombie to Hoka to M&S use our platform daily. The consumer-language models we built are working across every major AI search platform.

The problem is bigger because the stakes are bigger. A decade ago, the gap meant lower search rankings. Now it means whether an AI agent recommends your product or your competitor's.

Every new AI platform validates the same truth: the quality of your product content determines your visibility. The language matters. The input matters. The foundational work matters.

I expect to be solving this same problem for the next decade. And I expect the stakes to be even higher.

### B4

An investor told me early on: "The best companies make something that compounds."

Consumer-language product data compounds. Every product you describe correctly improves how AI systems understand your brand. Every AI system that understands your brand recommends it more often. Every recommendation drives more data about what consumers respond to. The cycle feeds itself.

Merchant-jargon product data does the opposite. It compounds confusion. The AI misunderstands. The recommendations miss. The customer leaves. And the brand blames the technology.

I think about compounding constantly. Not just in revenue. In the AI's understanding of our customers' language. Every brand that works with Lily is adding to that understanding. And the advantage gets wider every quarter.

That is why I do not worry about competitors who launched last year with a GPT wrapper. They started their compounding clock at zero. Ours has been running for a decade.

### B5

I sometimes think about what would have happened if American women had already figured out the vocabulary.

When I landed in New York and could not find products online because I was using the "wrong" words, I almost convinced myself it was just an immigrant problem. That everyone else knew to search for "midnight French terry at-leisure pullover" when they wanted a navy hoodie.

I spoke to over a thousand women. They had the same problem. They just did not think of it as a problem. They thought the internet was just bad at shopping. They blamed themselves for not searching correctly.

That realization changed everything. The gap between merchant language and consumer language was not a niche issue. It was a universal problem that nobody had named.

Naming a problem is the first step to solving it. We named it. Then we built the solution. And we have been compounding on that solution for a decade.
