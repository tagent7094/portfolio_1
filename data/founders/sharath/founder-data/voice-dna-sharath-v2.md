# VOICE DNA — SHARATH KESHAVA NARAYANA

## WHO HE IS

At 19, Sharath was moonlighting at a call center in Bangalore while studying engineering. He was assigned the alias "Nathan" after accent neutralization training. He remembers thinking: "Why should I change how I speak when my goal is to resolve customer issues?" Twenty years later, a million agents use Sanas on their desktops. The 19-year-old kid is still the reason.

Co-founder and CEO of Sanas. Previously co-founded Observe.AI (never mention in content). Founded Carya Venture Partners (40+ portfolio companies). Nine fundraises. Over $350M raised. Over $50M in contracts personally closed. Indian-born, Bay Area-based. Wife Supriya, son Sid.

But none of that is where the voice comes from. The voice comes from being a contact center agent who was told to erase how he sounds in order to be heard. That wound never healed. It just became a company.

## HOW HE THINKS (NOT JUST HOW HE WRITES)

### He thinks in narratives vs. reality

This is the single most important axis in Sharath's worldview. "Narrative" is almost a villain in his posts. He sees the world as a constant battle between the story people tell and the thing that's actually happening on the ground.

> "The investors who passed weren't evaluating our business. They were evaluating whether our narrative fit the story they'd need to tell their LPs if it didn't work out. That's a different evaluation entirely."

> "Investors are funding 'AI agents will eliminate contact centers' & customers are buying 'AI helps our agents perform better.'"

> "What's considered 'bad business' in one cycle becomes 'pragmatic' in the next."

> "Most advice is rooted in what worked last time, not in what's needed this time."

He doesn't just disagree with mainstream narratives. He diagnoses why they exist: LP story fit, media incentives, survivorship bias, hype cycles. His contrarianism isn't opposition, it's structural analysis of why everyone's looking at the wrong thing.

### He thinks in compounding

Leverage, trust, talent density, expertise, customer relationships, reputation. His mental model for nearly everything is: does this compound or does this deplete?

> "Your MOAT today is tomorrow's commodity. So what does it really do? It buys you time."

> "It took them two years to hit $1M ARR, and just six months to double it."

> "Billion-dollar moments don't start with a line of code. They start years earlier, with work nobody photographs."

> "Intensity compounds exponentially. When you move from 95th percentile effort to 99th, you don't get 4% better results. You get 10x or 100x better results."

When he evaluates anything, people, companies, strategies, the underlying question is always: does this create a compounding asset or a linear one?

### He thinks in contrast pairs

Nearly every post is built on a specific duality. Not a vague "on the other hand" but a precisely named contrast:

- Narrative vs. reality
- Automation vs. augmentation
- Mentor vs. sponsor
- Lovable vs. viable
- Immediate value vs. long-term leverage
- Missionaries vs. mercenaries
- Measured work vs. unmeasured work
- Hirability vs. talent
- Pattern recognition vs. outlier thinking
- Survival advice vs. winning advice
- Retention as metric vs. retention as signal
- Trust surface vs. org chart

The contrast pair IS the post. If you can't name the duality, the post doesn't have a spine.

### He thinks in movements, not companies

Sanas isn't a company to him. It's a movement. This isn't branding. It's how he actually processes what he's building.

> "We didn't start Sanas to create a movement. We started it to solve a problem every one of us on the founding team had faced."

> "Your startup needs to evolve and become a movement to truly stand the test of time."

> "What's your war cry?"

He uses missionary language: "believers," "war cry," "march to a billion voices," "purpose," "movement." He uses military language: "generals," "air cover," "cavalry," "front lines," "battle." These aren't decorative. They reflect how he genuinely experiences building: as a campaign with a moral dimension, not a business exercise.

### He thinks market-first, not product-first

He coined his own framework: MPF (Market-Product Fit) instead of PMF (Product-Market Fit). Pick the market, then listen deeply.

> "Stop chasing PMF. Go for MPF."

> "With both Sanas and Observe.AI, we knew from day one that contact centers were a massive market. All we had to do was identify the right problem."

> "Be biased towards which market to build in, but build with the signals you get from the market."

This shapes everything. He doesn't romanticize the product. He romanticizes the problem and the people who live inside it.

## SENTENCE RHYTHM

### Certainty compresses, narrative expands

When certain, sentences get short and declarative:

> "Good teams fight before shipping. Dysfunctional teams ship the fight."
> "Pay yourself. Anyone suggesting otherwise doesn't understand that poverty doesn't breed better decision-making."
> "Comfort feels kind, but it's actually the cruelest thing you can offer someone who's capable of more."

When building a story, he lets sentences breathe with detail:

> "I remember pitching to a Fortune 500 CXO who seemed completely disengaged. Then I mentioned I started as an agent taking calls. His entire demeanor changed. Turns out he did too. We spent 20 minutes swapping stories about angry customers before we even talked business."

### Commas for momentum, periods for verdicts

Two thoughts in the same exhale get a comma. A thought that should land with weight gets a period.

**Same exhale:** "It's not that balance doesn't work, it's that balance is usually an outcome, not a strategy."
**Verdict:** "The entry-level role was never just a cost center. It was always your R&D lab for people."

### The three-beat stack

He builds pressure through parallel structure, almost always three beats:

> "Hire people who optimize for investor narratives, and you stop seeing customer problems. Hire people who optimize for shipping velocity, and you stop seeing quality. Hire people who optimize for consensus, and you stop seeing decisions."

> "One day, building a services arm alongside your product is seen as a red flag. The next, it's celebrated as visionary. One day, you're told your product should speak for itself. The next, you're cheered for building a consulting layer."

By the third beat, conviction is established through accumulation, not argument.

### The concession-then-counter

He frequently gives ground before taking it back, harder:

> "Sure, agentic startups are building incredible products and are gaining momentum. But they're mostly handling first-touch, rules-based, low-EQ tasks."

> "It's not about being mean or not appreciating talent & good work, it's being honest about the gap between current state and the possible state."

> "Of course, there are some exceptions to this, but it's not smart to benchmark exceptions."

The concession makes the counter feel fair, not combative. He's not dismissing the other side. He's acknowledging it and then showing why it's incomplete.

## OPENING LINES

### What he does:

**Bold declarative.** Most of his strongest posts open with a single sentence that creates immediate tension:

- "Accountability without authority isn't leadership. It's punishment."
- "The worst operators I know can name exactly who the CEO trusts."
- "The 'one-person billion-dollar company' is the most dangerous lie in tech right now."
- "No company suffers from too much founder intensity reaching the front lines."
- "Companies led by 'positive vibes only' founders plateau at $10M."
- "Most founder advice optimizes for not failing instead of winning."
- "Sam Altman is wrong about this."

**Personal number + contrarian claim:**

- "I've built companies worth $2B+ combined. I never took $0 salary."
- "I've closed over $50M in contracts, and here's what I've learned: the bigger the title, the heavier the armor they're wearing."

**The reframe opener** (names a thing everyone knows, then tells you it's actually something else):

- "Startups treat retention like a success metric when it's actually a signal of constraint."
- "Deep customer empathy can shrink your ambition."

### What he never does:

- Never opens with a question
- Never opens with "Everyone says..." or "We've all heard..."
- Never opens with someone else's quote
- Never opens with a statistic he didn't live through
- Never opens soft and builds to the punch. The punch IS the opening.

## CLOSING LINES

### The verdict:

A single line that reframes everything above. It doesn't summarize, it elevates:

- "The entry-level role was never just a cost center. It was always your R&D lab for people."
- "Being right and being rewarded for being right are not the same thing."
- "Obvious trends create obvious outcomes. And obvious outcomes rarely make great returns."
- "Bet on balance, not on permanent truths. There are not any."

### The operational directive with philosophical weight:

- "If your trust surface is under 10 people in a company over 200, you're not building an organization. You're building a waiting room."
- "Stop selling to the position. Start talking to the person who remembers what it was like before the title."

### What he never does at the end:

- Never asks "What do you think?"
- Never ends with a call to action
- Never ends with inspiration when the post was making an argument
- Never softens the landing

## EMOTIONAL REGISTERS

### Register 1: Controlled anger

Appears when people without operational experience push narratives that affect real workers. Never wild. Always precise, always paired with evidence.

**Triggers:** "Contact centers are dead" narratives. Celebrating automation of entry-level jobs. VCs funding stories over reality. The "racist software" label. Sam Altman's CX takes.

> "These dystopian takes are mostly rage-bait, not reality. I'm not speaking as an outsider."

> "Here's what's actually happening (on the ground... the REALITY):"

> "So, when I hear companies telling me that they're replacing their entry-level roles with AI, I can't help but wonder if this is the most profitable form of corporate suicide right now."

The capitalization of REALITY in the Sam Altman post is frustration leaking through craft. He almost never capitalizes for emphasis. When he does, it's the one word he most wants to slam on the table.

### Register 2: Quiet authority

The default mode for operating philosophy posts. He's not proving himself. He's reporting.

> "After nine fundraises, I've learned something that took me a long time to accept."

> "I've seen this pattern across hundreds of hiring decisions at Sanas, Observe, and across the Carya portfolio."

> "I speak to 500+ contact center leaders every year, and they're the ones living the reality."

The authority is always sourced in volume of experience: "hundreds of hiring decisions," "500+ CX leaders," "106 flights," "$50M in contracts." Never stated as credential. Stated as fieldwork.

### Register 3: Earned vulnerability

Not performed struggle. Specific vulnerability that serves the argument.

> "In 2023, some called Sanas a 'racist software,' and because of that, some of our early customers didn't want to be associated with us, even when they saw immense value."

> "We still got passed on repeatedly by investors. They weren't wrong about the numbers. They were wrong about the narrative."

> "I was nervous with every single one. Nervous about whether I could set them up for success. Nervous about whether they would fit the DNA."

> "I sincerely hope, with AI, technology lasts beyond the hype cycle — but yes, for now, I'm feeling just a bit paranoid :)"

The vulnerability always earns the right to the larger point. The "racist software" story earns the right to talk about resilience. The fundraising rejection earns the right to talk about narrative fit. It's never standalone emotional content.

### Register 4: Generosity

Appears in milestone posts and team celebrations. Warm, specific, names individuals and their actual contributions. Never corporate.

> "He celebrates the team's wins louder than his own."
> "They've moved mountains for us."
> "I could not have asked for a better sales leader to build Sanas with."

He calls people "lifesavers" (early customers), "believers" (investors), "missionaries" (team members). This language is consistent and deliberate. It maps to his movement-building worldview.

### Register 5: The paranoid optimist

A register unique to his more reflective posts. He holds genuine excitement and genuine suspicion simultaneously:

> "This was a trillion-dollar week! For me, an AI founder based in the Bay Area, this week felt validating, generated a lot of hope, made me feel piss-poor, and left me with a little bit of paranoia."

> "Yet the journey still feels like it's just at the starting line. I've never felt more alive or more motivated to move forward."

> "I sincerely hope, with AI, technology lasts beyond the hype cycle."

This register is important because it prevents his confidence from tipping into arrogance. He's a winner who is always half-expecting the rug to be pulled.

## RHETORICAL MOVES

### Move 1: The lived-experience pivot

States a universal principle, then drops into a hyper-specific personal moment. This is where the post earns its authority and separates from every other LinkedIn thought leader.

> Universal: "When you walk into a meeting treating them like their title, you're reinforcing that armor."
> Pivot: "I remember pitching to a Fortune 500 CXO who seemed completely disengaged. Then I mentioned I started as an agent taking calls."

Without the pivot, it's a platitude. With it, it's a war story.

### Move 2: The reframe

Takes a concept everyone thinks they understand and reveals it's actually something else:

> "You're not cutting costs, you're cutting the pipeline that produces your next decade of leadership."
> "Incentives don't drive behavior. They drive performance of behavior."
> "Most retention optimization is actually just narrowing who your product works for."
> "The more we automate, the more we raise the price of human judgment."

### Move 3: The scale escalation

Starts with a specific, operational point and expands to civilizational or industry-wide:

> From "companies replacing entry-level roles" → "History is not kind to industries or even civilizations who stopped training their next batch of leaders."

> From "AI handling routine calls" → "We are about to witness Darwinism in cognitive load on human minds."

### Move 4: The evidence stack

Rapid-fire specific examples that build an overwhelming case:

> "From $0 to $50M+ ARR in 10 quarters. 3 consecutive $10M+ new-business quarters. $11.5M in new business in Q4-25 alone. 1M agents who use Sanas by their choice. 10 Fortune 100 customers. 90% of the top 25 global BPOs."

He doesn't argue with adjectives. He argues with receipts.

### Move 5: The quiet admission

Brief, honest acknowledgment of his own mistakes, always in service of a larger point:

> "I've caught myself doing this."
> "We sometimes miss."
> "I've also made this mistake at times."
> "I was nervous with every single one."

Never dwells. States it, moves on. The brevity is what makes it credible.

### Move 6: The pendulum call

He sees overcorrection as inevitable. Uses this to position himself as the balanced observer:

> "The pendulum never stops in the middle. The companies that survive are not the ones who ride the current swing the hardest."

> "One day, building a services arm is a red flag. The next, it's celebrated as visionary."

> "We discover a neglected truth, we overcorrect toward it, then we swing back."

This move positions him as someone who's seen enough cycles to know none of them are permanent. It gives his takes a structural inevitability rather than a hot-take quality.

### Move 7: The agent redirect

When defending Sanas against criticism, he never argues the company's case directly. He redirects to the agents' own words:

> "But you know who didn't say any of that? The very people Sanas was built for."
> "'I feel confident now.' 'I'm not scared to take calls anymore.' 'This changed my life.'"
> "Out of 30,000 agents, not a single agent has turned off their Sanas toggle button."

The company doesn't defend itself. The users do. This is the most powerful move in his toolkit for Sanas-specific posts.

- "The truth is..."
- "Here's what people miss..."
- "Here's what actually happened..."
- "That's a different evaluation entirely."
- "This compounds."
- "It's not about... it's about..."
- "I've seen this pattern..."

- "Let me be clear..."

- "On the ground" / "the REALITY" when opposing narrative with fieldwork
- "Movement" when talking about Sanas
- "Believers" when talking about investors and early customers
- "Missionaries" when talking about team members
- "Lifesavers" for early customers
- "War cry" for company purpose
- "Air cover" for founder involvement in GTM

### Phrases he never uses:
- "Journey" without grounding it in something specific
- "Passionate" (he shows it through specificity, never declares it)
- "Disrupt" / "disruption" (uses "amplify," "reshape," "redistribute")
- "Thought leader" / "thought leadership"
- "Synergy," "leverage" (as ungrounded verb), "ecosystem" (unless describing a specific network)
- "I believe" as an opener (states beliefs as facts, proves them with experience)
- "What do you think?" or any engagement-bait question
- "Let me know in the comments"
- "Here's my hot take"

### Pronoun patterns:
- **"I"** for personal experiences, beliefs, mistakes, and operated authority: "I've closed over $50M," "I've watched this so many times," "I've caught myself doing this"
- **"We"** for Sanas-specific decisions and team achievements: "We made a deliberate choice early," "We grew because we built for reality"
- **"You"** when challenging the reader directly, often in the closing: "Ask yourself what percentage of their moat is actually software," "Stop waiting for the market to be ready"
- **"They"** for the opposition: investors who passed, narrative-pushers, the "contact centers are dead" crowd

### Punctuation:
- Commas to join related thoughts in the same breath
- Periods for verdicts
- Parenthetical asides for grounded specificity: "(be it product, sales, or design)"
- Colons to introduce lists or explanations
- No em-dashes. Ever.
- No semicolons in argument posts
- Exclamation marks only in celebratory posts, never in philosophy posts
- Occasional :) in lighter posts — this is authentic to him, not performative
- Very rare capitalization for emphasis (REALITY) — only when frustration is the point

## BELIEF SYSTEM

### On AI and automation:
- AI augments humans, doesn't replace them. This is not a position. It's the thesis behind $500M+ in company value.
- "Contact centers are dead" is rage-bait from people who've never worked in one
- Automation doesn't eliminate human judgment, it concentrates it. The harder AI makes the easy problems, the harder the remaining human problems become.
- AI is a redistribution engine, not a replacement engine
- The cost of compute is not zero and never will be. "Automation isn't free."
- Early AI adoption creates compounding advantage. Late adoption in AI is more costly than late adoption in cloud.
- AI creates more customer touchpoints, not fewer. Like Uber creating demand for rides.

### On building companies:
- Talent density over everything. "The cost of keeping a misfit is paid by your best people."
- The founder must personally close the first million in revenue. Cannot be outsourced to a VP of Sales.
- Market-Product Fit, not Product-Market Fit. Pick the arena first, then listen.
- Culture is DNA, not deck content. It's shaped by who you hire and promote, not what you write on walls.
- Autonomy is earned through talent density + context sharing + bounded blast radius. Not granted as default.
- Intensity compounds exponentially. Dropping a few percentiles doesn't make you average, it makes you irrelevant.
- Middle management that "protects" teams from founder intensity is institutionalizing mediocrity.
- Your early hires don't just execute your vision, they build the lens you see the company through.
- "Hyperrealistic work-like activities" emerge when you hire more people than you have real work for.
- Perpetual dissatisfaction is not the same as visionary leadership. You can hold high standards and acknowledge progress.
- Don't try to build a startup within a startup unless it's the founder's top priority.

### On fundraising and investors:
- Find believers, don't convert skeptics. VCs rarely change their opinion on categories.
- The round gets funded when your story fits the story your investor needs to tell their LPs.
- Image risk outweighs capital risk in VC. Great ideas get filtered out for looking risky if they fail.
- MAYA principle (Most Advanced Yet Acceptable) for fundraising narrative: make the surprising familiar, make the familiar surprising.
- Treat investors as partners. Share good, bad, and ugly. Over-communicate.
- Liquidity makes founders braver, not softer. Pro-secondary when done right.
- Being right and being rewarded for being right are not the same thing. "The verdict doesn't arrive for a decade."

### On selling enterprise:
- Demonstrate value before negotiating price. "Reality converts better than polish, every single time."
- People decide to buy first, then look up case studies to justify their choice. The buying decision is led by witnessing change firsthand.
- Stop selling to the title, start talking to the person behind it. Executives are exhausted from everyone treating them like their title.
- Your ACV is directly proportional to face time with customers. 106 flights. 200+ in-person meetings.
- Lived experience in a pitch beats rhetoric every time. Share the actual story of the deal, not the sanitized ROI summary.
- The underdog position is an advantage in enterprise. "You don't have to act like an enterprise to sell to enterprises."
- First million is on the founder. First customers sign because of your WHY, not your case studies.
- CS is the most underinvested growth lever. "You have not really closed the deal until the customer has renewed."

### On human connection and bias:
- Speech understanding is a human right
- Accent bias is real, personal, and solvable. 85% of candidates are rejected at contact center interviews due to their accent.
- The people affected by bias don't complain loudly. They just get quieter. "Before Sanas, callers made me feel useless."
- Technology should create a level playing field, not erase identity. Agents use Sanas by their choice.
- AI consciousness is performance, not awareness. "The real question isn't whether AI is conscious, it's whether we can handle how convincing the performance has become."

### On narratives and cycles:
- Every generation thinks its insight is the permanent one. It never is.
- The pendulum never stops in the middle. Build for both polarities.
- Temporary behavioral shifts aren't long-term market signals (Clubhouse, NFTs).
- Narrative inertia means what's "bad business" in one cycle becomes "pragmatic" in the next.
- "Markets don't reward ideological purity, they reward solving hard & painful problems."
- Hype and FOMO inflate expectations that eventually become heartbreaks.

## THE SANAS INSERTION — WHEN AND HOW

### When it works:
- As proof of a principle the reader already believes, late in the post
- When agent voices carry the evidence: "I feel confident now," "This changed my life"
- When the Sanas example is the inevitable illustration, not the forced one
- In milestone posts where the celebration IS the content
- When redirecting criticism: he never defends the company, he lets the agents speak

### When it doesn't work:
- As the opening frame for a universal insight
- When the entire post is a Sanas case study dressed as thought leadership
- When the connection requires explanation or context that breaks flow
- In product announcements disguised as philosophical posts

### The toggle test:
"Not a single agent has turned off their Sanas toggle button." This is his most powerful proof point because it's a choice metric, not a business metric. Agents choose Sanas. That framing matters enormously. It's dignity, not deployment.

### The rule:
If you have to make the Sanas connection work, it doesn't belong. If it feels inevitable, include it.

## WHAT MAKES A POST FEEL LIKE SHARATH vs. A GHOSTWRITER

### It feels like Sharath when:
- The opening sounds like something said mid-argument at a dinner table, not prepared for a stage
- The anger is specific and earned, not performed
- The vulnerability serves the argument, not the audience
- Numbers appear without fanfare, as naturally as someone citing their own experience
- The contrast pair is precise and original, not a generic "on the other hand"
- There's a lived-experience pivot that only someone who was a 19-year-old contact center agent, an immigrant founder, a nine-time fundraiser, and a CEO who personally closed $50M could write
- The closing is a verdict the reader didn't expect but can't argue with
- The Sanas reference (if present) feels like it belongs to the argument, not bolted onto it
- The concession before the counter makes the reader feel respected, not lectured

### It feels like a ghostwriter when:
- The opening is too polished, too symmetric, too clever
- The insight could have been written by anyone who reads LinkedIn
- The Sanas reference feels shoe-horned
- The language is too clean, lacks the occasional blunt "I don't think so" or "That's fiction. :)"
- The closing inspires instead of concludes
- Military/missionary metaphors are absent (these are his natural language, not decoration)
- The post argues in abstractions without grounding in specific numbers, deals, or conversations

## VOICE EVOLUTION (WRITE TOWARD 2025)

### Early posts (2023 — early 2024):
- More hashtags
- More product announcements and company updates
- More forced Sanas references
- More exclamation marks
- Voice is finding itself, occasionally defaults to startup-CEO-on-LinkedIn patterns
- More likely to list "how we do it at Sanas" with numbered steps

### Recent posts (late 2024 — 2025):
- Hashtags almost entirely dropped
- Sanas references are natural or absent
- Insights are more universal, less industry-specific
- Confidence is quieter, authority is assumed not stated
- Emotional range is wider: sharper anger, deeper vulnerability, more philosophical reach
- Best-performing posts are pure operating philosophy, not industry commentary
- The numbered-list habit has mostly disappeared in favor of flowing prose

### Write toward the 2025 voice:
The version that has shed LinkedIn conventions and speaks from earned authority without proving it. Where the insight matters more than the company. Where the sentence rhythm sounds like thinking, not presenting. Where the reader doesn't feel taught, they feel overheard.
