# Indian Education System Talent Selection Bias: What AI Exposes

The Indian education system doesn't produce talent — it produces people who are talented at passing exams.

This is something I think about as a founder, not as a policy critic. The Indian education system talent selection bias shapes who shows up in our interview rooms, what they're optimized for, and what they can't do. It's not an equity problem. It's a value-creation problem — one that compounds through every team you build, every role you fill, every leader you try to develop.

Here's what brought it back into focus. I was on a call recently, and I said something I've believed for years but rarely say out loud: "JEE is one of the longest-running scams in the country." The room went quiet. Not because people disagreed — because they didn't know how to process someone saying it plainly.

So let me say it plainly here. Not as a hot take — as someone who's built companies on both sides of this system and seen what it produces.

## The Longest-Running Scam Nobody Questions

Here's what people miss about JEE Advanced entrance exam and the IIT selection criteria built around it — no university in the world boasts about their entrance examination. MIT doesn't. Stanford doesn't. Oxford doesn't. But when it comes to JEE, we boast that we're the best.

Think about that. We've built an entire national identity around a filter — not what comes after the filter, not what students build, not what they create — just the filter itself.

The Kota coaching culture is the clearest expression of this. Hundreds of thousands of students every year relocating to a single city to optimize for a single test. Not to learn physics. Not to fall in love with mathematics. To crack an exam. The Indian engineering curriculum downstream of this moment is shaped by it — students arrive already trained to optimize, already wired to treat knowledge as a means to a score.

This compounds.

When those students enter the workforce, they bring the same orientation. Crack the interview. Switch to a higher-paying job. Finance the lifestyle through EMI. "This entire system is connected — degrees, jobs, EMI — everything is linked. We can't just replace it overnight." But we do need to name it.

## What Is the Crack Mentality in Indian Education?

> **The "crack mentality"** is the orientation — embedded across Indian education from JEE to NEET medical entrance exam preparation — that treats every milestone as a gate to be cracked through optimization, not as depth-based learning or genuine mastery. It rewards speed of pattern recognition over comfort with ambiguity.

The crack mentality in Indian education hiring impact is massive and largely invisible. When I hire, I see it immediately. Candidates who spent their formative years cracking exams are spectacular at structured problems with known solutions. Give them an ambiguous problem — something where the answer isn't in the back of the textbook — and you see the hesitation.

That hesitation is the Indian education system talent selection bias made visible.

The beautiful thing about computer science and AI is that almost every industry needs AI people. But the AI skill requirements for today's world aren't about pattern-matching — they're about comfort with uncertainty, ability to build under constraints, willingness to ship something imperfect and iterate. The crack mentality selects against every single one of these traits.

If something that took you 10 days to build in software now takes you more than one day, you're not fit for today's world. The people best prepared for that compression aren't the ones who optimized their way through entrance exams — they're the ones who spent years building things nobody asked them to build.

## Why Does the Indian Education System Filter Out Builders?

Because filtering for builders is expensive, messy, and illegible to institutions that need legibility.

Here's what separates builders from optimizers: builders tolerate ambiguity. They sit with a problem longer than is comfortable. They ship before they're ready. They define the problem before solving it — the opposite of what every entrance exam teaches, where the problem is already defined and your only job is to apply the right formula faster than the next person.

The startup founder hiring patterns I've observed over two decades confirm this. The best founders I know — the ones who actually build things that matter — almost never describe their education as the formative experience. They describe a side project, a failed business, a random job.

When I was 19, I was moonlighting at a call center while studying in Bangalore. That entry-level job didn't just provide income — it gave me the problem I'd spend the next 20 years solving, the problem that eventually led to a $50M ARR company. No entrance exam predicted that. No IIT selection criteria would have surfaced it.

Meanwhile, the system keeps producing optimizers and calling them talent. It's not talent — it's hirability.

## Builder vs Optimizer — Why the Distinction Matters for AI Readiness

The builder vs optimizer mindset distinction isn't about intelligence, it's about orientation.

| Dimension | Optimizer | Builder |
|---|---|---|
| Relationship to ambiguity | Avoids it — seeks defined problems | Seeks it — defines problems themselves |
| Education signal | High test scores, pedigree institutions | Projects, failures, self-directed learning |
| Career pattern | Crack → Switch → EMI | Depth → Build → Compound |
| Response to AI disruption | Threatened — AI optimizes better | Amplified — AI accelerates building |
| Value in startups | Execution on known paths | Creating new paths |
| Talent density contribution | Moderate — needs structure | High — creates structure for others |

Every dimension in this table maps back to the same root cause — a system that optimized for one type and called it universal.

This is why high test scores don't predict builder talent. The optimization that produces high scores is the exact muscle AI commoditizes first. AI disruption and Indian education system readiness are on a collision course — the system mass-produces the skill set that's becoming obsolete.

We need to move from "Crack, Switch, EMI" to "Depth, Build, Compound." That's not a slogan — that's contingency planning in education for a world where compounding knowledge systems matter more than credentialed shortcuts.

## What I Learned Hiring Across Both Worlds

After experiencing the Indian education system talent selection bias from both sides — after watching offshoring talent trends reshape the pipeline, after hiring hundreds from traditional and non-traditional backgrounds — here's what I know:

1. **Trust-based hiring outperforms credential-based hiring every time.** When you hire on trust — on demonstrated judgment, on evidence of building — you get people who create value in ambiguous environments. When you hire on credentials, you get people who are excellent at joining organizations. Not the same thing.

2. **Talent density in startups depends entirely on what you select for.** If your filter rewards optimization, your team will be full of optimizers. Predictable work, no breakthroughs. High talent density requires builders — people who create structure, not just follow it.

3. **Market product fit vs product market fit is a depth problem.** MPF requires the kind of depth-based learning the crack mentality never develops. You can't crack your way to market product fit. You have to live in the problem long enough that the solution becomes obvious.

4. **Depth vs crack mentality in talent selection is the single highest-leverage hiring decision a founder makes.** I'll take someone with five years of depth in one domain over someone with five credentials across five domains, every single time.

5. **AI makes this worse before it makes it better.** Cognitively agile talent is more crucial than ever as AI commoditizes busywork. How entrance exams predict entrepreneurial ability is about to become an urgent question — and the answer won't be flattering.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How does JEE preparation impact startup founder success?

JEE coaching culture and startup founder success rates are inversely correlated in my experience — not because JEE students aren't smart, but because the preparation trains structured problem-solving under time pressure while atrophying the muscles that matter for founders: comfort with ambiguity, contrarian thinking, builder instinct. The Indian education system talent selection bias embedded in JEE filters for a type well-suited for large organizations and poorly suited for the zero-to-one phase of building.

### Is the Crack, Switch, EMI model still viable in 2024?

No. AI compresses the value of optimization-based work. Offshoring talent trends are shifting toward depth over cost arbitrage. The EMI-funded lifestyle that the switching game supports requires salary growth that isn't guaranteed for roles where AI replicates 80% of the output.

### How should education prepare students for AI-driven work?

Contingency planning in education for the AI era means building systems that produce builders, not optimizers. Depth-based learning over breadth-based cramming. Compounding knowledge systems where each year builds on the last instead of resetting for the next entrance exam.

### What does depth-based learning mean for hiring?

Stop screening for credentials. Start screening for evidence of sustained engagement with hard problems. Depth-based learning produces people with domain intuition — not just domain knowledge. In hiring, that shows up as first-principles reasoning rather than case-study pattern-matching.
