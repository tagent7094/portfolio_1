	At 19, I was moonlighting at a call center while studying in Bangalore, India. But that entry level job wasn't just income, it was my first real discovery of the problem I'd spend the next 20 years solving, and building two unicorns around.

When you automate away the bottom, you don't get a leaner company. You get a company with a missing generation.


The entry-level role was never just a cost center. It was always your R&D lab for people.

	Everyone is wrong about which incumbents AI will kill.


The conventional wisdom: the companies everyone writes off as too slow to adapt are AI's primary victims. Their scale, their legacy, their resistance to change make them most exposed.


That logic ignores what actually makes a business defensible.


Technology is roughly 5% of most companies' moat. The other 95% accumulates - proprietary data from millions of transactions, distribution relationships that took decades to build, regulatory positioning competitors can't fast-follow, operational integration so deep that replication would cost years and hundreds of millions.


AI replicates the 5%. It doesn't touch the 95%.


So disruption isn't uniform. Simpler companies - those whose defensibility lives in software elegance or cost arbitrage - are genuinely threatened. Their moat is exactly what AI commoditizes. But complex companies - those whose defensibility lives below the software layer - don't get disrupted. They get amplified. AI hands them analytical capability without giving any attacker access to the structural conditions that made them defensible in the first place.


The companies most likely to die from AI are the simple ones. The ones most likely to win are the ones everyone assumed would lose.


So before you bet against an old, slow "legacy" company - ask yourself what percentage of their moat is actually software. Then ask how exposed the rest of it is.

This doesn't mean all aggressive bets are good, it means the frame is wrong. Advice that helps you not die is different from advice that helps you build something nonlinear. The first comes from seeing lots of companies, the second comes from being inside one while it worked.


The founders who listen too carefully to pattern-based advice end up building reasonable companies with reasonable outcomes. Nothing breaks, nothing explodes, nothing matters.


If your board keeps steering you toward de-risking, ask whether they're optimizing for your outcome or their portfolio shape.

	Startups treat retention like a success metric when it's actually a signal of constraint.


High retention doesn't mean you built something great, it means you're serving a specific type of customer well. Low retention doesn't mean the product is broken, it means you're attracting people the product wasn't designed for.


The dangerous part is when leadership optimizes for the metric instead of the insight. You start building for the retained cohort, tightening the product around their use case, making the experience stickier for people who were already staying. This feels like progress because retention improves, but you're just making the product fit fewer people.


Meanwhile, the growth motion keeps pulling in customers who don't match the tightened profile, so churn stays high despite all the retention work. The problem isn't that you're not retaining enough, it's that your acquisition and product are solving for different people.


Most retention optimization is actually just narrowing who your product works for. You're not getting better at serving the market, you're getting better at serving the people who already fit.


If your retention work isn't changing who you acquire, you're optimizing the wrong layer.

So choose investors who understand your business. Not just investors who are fluent in your narrative.

	AI is the electrical grid moment for cognition.


Before electricity, capability was local. You could do what you personally could do or what animals and humans near you could do.


After electricity, capability became distributed. You accessed industrial levels of kinetic energy anywhere.


Likewise, before AI, cognitive capability was local. You could think what you personally could think or what specialists you hired could think. But after AI, cognitive capability becomes distributed. You access sophisticated analysis in any domain from anywhere.


The constraint changed from "can I afford a specialist?" to "do I know what problem to solve?"


Those whose value was "I have knowledge you don't" are being commoditized. That knowledge is now broadly accessible through AI.


Those whose value is "I know which problems matter, and how to direct AI at them" are becoming disproportionately valuable.


With AI, the cognitive grid is being built right now. The winners won't be the ones with exclusive knowledge. They'll be the ones with judgment and direction.

	Nobody talks about why learning velocity actually matters.


It's not because the field moves fast, it's because the field moves in unpredictable directions.


If AI was just moving fast in a straight line, experienced people would catch up. They'd learn the new tools, update their frameworks, and their pattern recognition would still apply.


But AI doesn't move in a straight line. It jumps sideways. Entire approaches become obsolete overnight while adjacent approaches you dismissed suddenly become the only thing that works.


That's the actual skill: not learning, but unlearning. Discarding knowledge you worked hard to acquire because it's now actively hurting you.


Most people can't do this. Their identity is wrapped up in what they know. Admitting their expertise is obsolete feels like admitting they're obsolete.


The people who thrive treat knowledge like inventory. It has carrying costs. Old inventory doesn't just sit there doing nothing - it takes up space where new inventory should go.

	Every person you hire wants to hire someone below them.


It's not evil, it's rational. Everyone knows headcount = compensation, influence & status. So hiring accelerates exponentially while actual valuable work grows slowly. Eventually, a company develops an internal economy where the supply of real work cannot keep up with the demand for work.


Result: people create "hyperrealistic work-like activities" like pre-meetings, deck reviews, analyses of marginal improvements. These activities look identical to real work but produce zero value.


So, your biggest problem isn't being understaffed. It’s being overstaffed with people who must invent fake work to justify their existence.


Most founders miss this (I've also made this mistake at times). They hire great people but create no meaningful work for them. And no high-performer sits idle, they generate complexity: meetings about meetings, decks previewing other decks, analyses where the cost exceeds the maximum possible benefit.


And this isn't a people problem. It’s a supply-demand mismatch: you hired 15 people but only have 8 people’s worth of real work. The remaining 7 will, inevitably, manufacture activity.


Our job as is identifying what actually matters, say no to everything else, create clarity so talented people aren't trapped "performing" fake work.

Narratives change, good businesses endure.
