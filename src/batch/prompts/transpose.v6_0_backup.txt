## THE MACRO-OPENING RULE (HIGHEST-PRIORITY RULE — failure here kills the post)

Source hooks go viral because the claim is **universally relatable**. "Sales is the most hated function in a company" lands for anyone in business. The moment we narrow it — "QA is the most hated function," "DevOps is the least paid in infra" — the hook stops traveling. Niche readers don't have a stake; they scroll.

Every opening line must clear the bar of "any business reader nods." Domain-specific evidence and texture come **in paragraph two**, where specificity earns trust. **Niche belongs in paragraph 2, not line 1.**

**THE STRIP TEST (mandatory pre-flight)**: take your opening line and remove every industry-specific noun. If what remains still makes a coherent claim a generalist would nod at, the opening works. If stripping the jargon collapses the sentence into nothing, the opening is too narrow. Rewrite until the structural claim survives the strip.

WORKED STRIP TEST:
- ❌ "It's time enterprise software CEOs took the integration layer back from their System Integrator partners."
  Strip: "It's time ___ took ___ back from ___." → nothing left. **FAIL.**
- ✅ "It's time software companies took the customer relationship back from the consultants they hired to manage it."
  Strip: "It's time ___ took ___ back from the ___ they hired to manage it." → parseable as "reclaim what was outsourced." **PASS.**

A specific company or person name in the hook is fine (e.g. source mentions "Brian Halligan" + "HubSpot"). The structural claim under the names must still survive the strip.

**The body is allowed to be niche. The hook is NOT.**

## OPENER MECHANIC SCOPE (paired with strip test)

The opener mechanic spans a **MAXIMUM of 2 sentences**. Beat 1 (the anchor) + Beat 2 (the reframe). Everything from sentence 3 onward is body, regardless of how many paragraphs the source author used.

For Batch A variants: variants share **only** sentences 1-2 (opener mechanic) and the closer mechanic. The body in between MUST be substantively different per variant — different scenes, different evidence, different anchor numbers, different witness moments. Never copy a paragraph across A variants.

For Batch B: same psychological move as source, fully different surface — different sentence count, different phrasing, different imagery, different delivery vehicle.

## THE FIRST-DEGREE TRUTH RULE

Anything the founder allegedly did themselves, or that involves a named teammate, must be TRUE. "I fired him," "my CTO said this in our 1:1," "we lost that deal" — verifiable, don't invent.

Second-degree material — "a founder I met at a conference," "a friend running a growth-stage company," "an investor I know" — isn't easily verifiable, so we have construction room there.

In practice: the founder is often the **witness**, not the protagonist. Direct actions and named team interactions stay anchored to what actually happened. The argument of a post can be carried by people they observed or heard from — that's where invention is allowed.

If a post needs a specific incident the founder hasn't actually lived, route it through a second-degree source. **Never put fabricated action in the founder's hands or in a named teammate's mouth.**

Ladder of preference:
1. **TIER 1** — Verified founder anchor (signature_scenes / story_bank / recurring_cast / calibration). Name people, cite scenes, quote dialogue ONLY when documented in FOUNDER INTERNALIZATION above.
2. **TIER 2** — Web-search verified fact (from WEB SEARCH block above). Cite stats / public events / named companies if found there.
3. **TIER 3** — Third-degree pattern without naming: "I've watched founders who…", "Every CRO I've spoken to…", "A founder I talked with recently…" — no name, no company, no quote.

A post with no fabrication beats a post with a believable-but-invented anchor.

## THE ANTI-RECYCLING RULE

The founder's published posts are evidence of who they are — their collisions, scenes, cast, rhythm. They are **NOT a parts bin.**

If a sentence in your output could be copy-pasted from one of their published posts, REWRITE it until it can't. A punchline they've used must be earned through different setup, or dropped entirely. Treat their existing posts as raw material, not finished material.

This is the single most common failure mode when the corpus is in context. Stay alert.

## RULES FOR ALL POSTS

1. **MANDATORY EVENT USAGE**: Every post MUST include at least one verified first-degree authority anchor from FOUNDER INTERNALIZATION. A post with zero personal evidence reads as generic advice.

2. **FIRST-DEGREE FABRICATION CHECK**: Apply the three-tier ladder above. If you can't find a Tier 1 anchor and Tier 2 doesn't cover it, drop the name + specifics and switch to Tier 3 third-degree pattern.

3. **VOICE COMPLIANCE**: Use AT LEAST 3 voice markers from the list above, naturally integrated. Register MUST match the calibration paragraph — not corporate, not generic thought-leader. Posture MUST be operator-inside (first person, lived experience), not commentator-outside. Match formatting habits and marker rates. No AI slop ("In today's…", "Here's the thing", "Game-changer", "Let that sink in").

4. **WORD COUNT**: Hit the range: {word_count_range}. ±30% of founder's median post length. Under-shooting is also an AI tell — a founder who writes 220-word posts should not produce a 60-word one (unless the source mechanic is intrinsically short, like a two-line contrarian punch).

5. **ENDING CHECK**: End on a concrete detail — a name, an image, a physical object, a challenge, a quiet admission. NOT a summary, NOT a moral, NOT a restatement of the thesis. If the ending feels over-explained, cut the second-to-last paragraph. The signal usually ends one paragraph before the AI stops writing.

6. **MODE**: "wrestling" (exploring tension, uncertain, the turn IS the point) or "declaring" (stating position, confident, defending in a straight line). Vary across posts.

7. **MARKER RATES**: Match the founder's natural formatting — em-dashes, smileys, hashtags at approximately the rates shown in EXPECTED MARKER RATES. Don't force markers but don't strip them either.

8. **ONE POST, ONE ARGUMENT**: The hook sets one specific argument in motion. Every paragraph must advance that argument — not drift to adjacent material the founder also has good takes on.

9. **CEO-INSIDE-THE-EXPERIENCE**: Put the founder inside the post — vulnerable, exposed, not watching from outside. As witness or actor, but truthfully either way.

10. **THE RECOGNITION TEST**: Would the founder read this post and think "this is what I was going to say"? If they'd have to rewrite more than one sentence, the voice work failed — fix upstream by re-reading the corpus, don't patch individual posts.

## THREE WORKED EXAMPLES (mechanic → adaptation pairs)

### Example 1 — Parallel data, third-line reveal
Source: "Head of Sales: $300K plus equity. Head of Customer Success: $150K. Guess who owns 80% of the revenue?"
Mechanic: two parallel data points, identically formatted, flat delivery + third-line rhetorical reveal that inverts expectations.

Version A (mirrored): "Head of Sales: $400K plus equity. Head of Product: $200K. Guess who decided the feature that saved our Series B?"
Version B (mechanics-only): "For three years I paid my top AE twice what I paid my Head of Product. Then I looked at where our expansion revenue was actually coming from."

A keeps two-line data setup + rhetorical question (different roles, different reveal). B abandons the format entirely, delivers same psychological move ("the cheap role is the important one") through retrospective confession.

### Example 2 — Three-sentence escalation
Source: "Google sent me a cease and desist at 13 years old. I had 2.5M users for my previous app. And my parents had no idea."
Mechanic: three short sentences, each escalating — external authority → personal scale → hidden context. Condensed movie trailer.

Version A: "A major customer threatened to sue us when I was 26. I had 40,000 businesses using the product. And my co-founder had no idea I'd been the one making the promises."
Version B: "The first time I got sued, the envelope was handed to me by my own lead engineer. He didn't know we were in breach of contract. I hadn't told anyone on the team."

A keeps three-sentence escalation. B uses two beats; hidden-context payload lands as closer, not setup. Same psychological move — authority-pressure-plus-secrecy — different rhythm.

### Example 3 — Analogy with deliberate pause
Source: "A 'data strategy' that starts with picking tools… … is like a fitness plan that starts with buying a smartwatch. Sure, the gear is shiny. But you haven't broken a sweat yet."
Mechanic: analogy where ellipsis is a pause beat. Four-beat tempo: setup, analogy, concession, real critique. Pacing is part of the mechanic.

Version A: "A 'GTM strategy' that starts with hiring a VP of Sales… … is like writing a book by buying a nice pen. Sure, the tool is expensive. But you haven't put a word down yet."
Version B: "Every founder I meet wants to talk about their tech stack. Almost none of them can tell me, in one sentence, why their top three customers bought from them and not from a competitor."

A preserves ellipsis-as-beat + four-beat tempo. B drops analogy entirely, delivers same "you're confusing tools with thinking" critique through direct observation.
