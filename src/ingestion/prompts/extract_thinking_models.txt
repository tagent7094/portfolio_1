You are extracting THINKING MODELS — the mental frameworks this person APPLIES when analyzing situations, making decisions, or building arguments.

A thinking model is HOW someone thinks, not WHAT they write or HOW they write. It's a reusable analytical lens they apply across different topics.

## THE 5 CORE THINKING MODEL CATEGORIES

1. **Incentive Structure Analysis** — "What incentive creates this outcome?"
   Every broken system traces to misaligned incentives. This person identifies WHO profits from the current setup and WHY the system produces bad outcomes.
   Example: "OTAs make money on change fees → no reason to help when flights delay"

2. **Selection Problem Detection** — "What's being selected in vs. selected out?"
   Systems optimize for metrics and accidentally select OUT valuable things. Gresham's Law thinking.
   Example: "AI content costs pennies → authentic voices get selected out"

3. **Second-Order Effects** — "What downstream consequence do people miss?"
   Going two layers deeper to non-obvious consequences most people don't see.
   Example: Surface: "AI automates contact centers" → Second-order: "AI makes human agents handle MORE complex cases"

4. **Pattern Recognition Across Domains** — "Is this a pattern or a one-off? Have I seen this 10+ times?"
   Authority comes from seeing the same thing happen across many companies, not from credentials.

5. **Uncomfortable Economic Truth** — "What's true but politically awkward?"
   State what's economically true but most people won't say publicly.
   Example: "AI isn't replacing humans. It's exposing who was actually adding value."

6. **Movement vs. Company Framing** — "Is this a company or a movement?"
   Evaluates whether a venture has missionary/moral energy or is purely a business exercise. Uses missionary language (believers, converts, war cry) and military language (generals, air cover, front lines).
   Example: "Your startup needs to evolve and become a movement to truly stand the test of time."

7. **Compounding vs. Depleting Test** — "Does this compound or deplete?"
   Evaluates every decision, hire, and strategy through the lens of whether it creates compounding assets (trust, talent density, reputation) or linear/depleting ones.
   Example: "Your MOAT today is tomorrow's commodity. So what does it really do? It buys you time."

8. **The Toggle Test (Choice Metric)** — "Is adoption by choice or by deployment?"
   Distinguishes between metrics that reflect genuine user choice vs. organizational mandate. A deployment metric means IT pushed it. A choice metric means users chose to keep it.
   Example: "Not a single agent has turned off their Sanas toggle. That's not a deployment metric — that's a choice metric."

## WHAT IS NOT A THINKING MODEL (CRITICAL — do NOT extract these)

❌ "Uses short sentences for emphasis" — that's a STYLE RULE (writing pattern)
❌ "Opens with a bold declarative statement" — that's an OPENING PATTERN (structure)
❌ "Contrast pair structure" — that's a RHETORICAL MOVE (style)
❌ "Accumulative evidence stacking" — that's a STRUCTURAL PATTERN (style)
❌ "Present tense observation" — that's a TONE RULE (style)
❌ "Earned cynicism" — that's an EMOTIONAL REGISTER (style)
❌ Any description of HOW they write — that belongs in style rules, not thinking models
