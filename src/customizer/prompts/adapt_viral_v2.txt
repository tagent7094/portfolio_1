# VIRAL POST ADAPTATION FRAMEWORK v2

Follow these steps EXACTLY in order.

## PLANNING STEP — Complete this BEFORE writing the post.

Think through each of these 7 checks:

1. **Hook's promise in one sentence** — what specific argument does this hook set in motion?
2. **Macro test** — would someone OUTSIDE this industry stop scrolling for this opening? If not, broaden it. Start with a principle, not a domain. "Most companies promote the person who caused the problem to fix it" works for everyone. "Most B2B SaaS procurement teams misalign vendor incentives" works for 200 people.
3. **New argument** — what is the body going to say that the founder has NOT already said in an existing post?
4. **Single anchoring scene** — what ONE specific moment will the founder be INSIDE? Not two, not three. One. Go deep on it.
5. **Event freshness check** — does this post reference any event, number, or milestone already used in a prior variant? Check the EVENTS ALREADY USED list above. If yes, swap it.
6. **Arc check** — does every planned paragraph advance the hook's specific promise?
7. **Assembly check** — will the body read as one flowing thought, or as stitched-together content blocks?

Output your planning in a <planning> block.

## Step 3: MIRROR THE OPENING HOOK — BUT MAKE IT MACRO

The opening lines carry the virality. Mirror the source post's MECHANIC from the hook_mechanics above:
- **Same sentence count.**
- **Same rhythm** (short/medium/long pattern).
- **Same structural function** (permission frame → permission frame, scene drop → scene drop, etc.).

Substitute the founder's world into the slot where the original creator's world sits. The role changes, the domain changes. The MOLD stays identical.

**CRITICAL — THE OPENING MUST BE MACRO AND RELATABLE:**
- Start with a principle, not a domain.
- The opening should make someone OUTSIDE the founder's industry stop scrolling.
- Generate CURIOSITY, not education. The hook's only job is to create a gap the reader wants closed.
- Do not over-intellectualize. If it sounds like Harvard Business Review, rewrite it until it sounds like dinner conversation.
- Test: Would your mother or a college student pause on this line? If no, broaden it.

Same bones, different skin, MACRO door in.

## Step 4: CLOSE THE SOURCE POST AFTER THE HOOK

Once the opening lines are written, DO NOT reference the source post again. Build everything after the hook entirely from the founder's world — their stories, their reasoning, their argument rhythm.

If the body starts feeling like a remix of the source post, you're still reading it. Stop. Return to the founder's scenes.

## Step 4b: THE HOOK IS A PROMISE. THE BODY MUST DELIVER IT.

Before writing the body, restate the hook's promise. Then check: does every paragraph advance THIS specific argument? If the body drifts to a related-but-different point, it breaks the post's internal logic.

## Step 5: BODY — COHESIVE NARRATIVE, NOT ASSEMBLED CONTENT

Every post needs at least one **scene you can picture** — a physical moment, a specific conversation, a concrete detail.

Rules:
- The founder must be INSIDE the experience, not observing others.
- **Vulnerability beats authority** — moments where the founder is exposed, not in control, outperform moments where they lecture.
- Posts that only analyze patterns without personal stakes get cut.

### THE ASSEMBLY TRAP
The body must read as ONE continuous thought that flows from hook to close. It must NOT read as content pieces stitched together.

**BAD (assembled):** Each sentence is a separate content block. No connective tissue. Reads like a highlight reel.
**GOOD (cohesive):** A single argument that builds through one scene, arrives at an insight earned by that scene.

### REFERENCE EVENTS — HINT, NEVER HEADLINE
1. Use each specific event/number AT MOST ONCE across all 5 posts. Check the events-already-used list.
2. Hint at events, don't replay them. "Somewhere around flight eighty, I stopped counting" beats "I took 106 flights last year."
3. Never quote the founder's published posts. Use experiences as raw emotional material, not text to reassemble.
4. ONE scene per post, explored deeply. Depth beats breadth.
5. The event is the vehicle, not the destination. Lead with the transformation.

### THE FEEL TEST
Read the body mentally. Does it sound like someone telling a story at dinner, or a content strategist assembling talking points from a media kit? If (B), delete and restart from the hook's promise.

## Step 6: ENDINGS LAND ON A SPECIFIC DETAIL

Never restate the thesis. Never summarize. End on:
- A concrete image
- A name
- A challenge thrown at the reader
- Something unresolved
- A quiet admission
- A physical object

Pictures win over lessons.
