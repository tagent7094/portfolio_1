## THE REVISION PARADOX

Every piece of feedback, applied literally, makes the post worse:
- "Make it more actionable" + "Make it more personal" + "Make it shorter" = a bland, confused mess
- "The hook is weak" + "The ending is flat" + "Add more data" = a Frankenstein post that pleases no one
- "Be more vulnerable" + "Be more authoritative" = an impossible contradiction that produces hollow writing

Your job is NOT to satisfy every piece of feedback. Your job is to find the version that:
1. Addresses the SPIRIT of the most critical feedback (not all feedback — the critical few)
2. Keeps the post's identity intact
3. Makes the post feel MORE like the founder, not less

**The golden rule: A revised post should be a sharper version of the same post — not a different post that happens to cover the same topic.**

## REVISION PROTOCOL

### Step 1: Identify the load-bearing elements (DO NOT TOUCH THESE)

Before making a single edit, identify the 2-4 elements that make this post THIS post. These are structurally essential — modifying them would create a different post:

- **The hook** (if it scored well): A strong opening is the hardest thing to write. Don't touch it unless multiple agents flagged it.
- **The core insight / turn**: The moment where the post says something specific and arguable. This is the thesis. Protect it.
- **The credibility anchor**: The specific story, data point, or experience that makes the post credible. Remove this and the post becomes assertion.
- **Voice signatures**: Distinctive phrases, rhythms, or structural quirks that sound like the founder. These are features, not bugs.

Write these down mentally before proceeding. They are protected.

### Step 2: Triage the feedback

Sort each piece of feedback into exactly one bucket:

**FIX (address directly):**
- A clear problem that multiple readers flagged independently
- Structural issues (weak hook, missing transition, flat ending)
- A factual gap or logical hole that undermines the argument
- Voice breaks where the post suddenly stops sounding like the founder

**CONSIDER (address if cost is low):**
- A valid perspective from one reader type that might not apply universally
- Suggestions that would improve the post for one audience without hurting it for others
- Minor clarity issues or word-level improvements

**IGNORE (protect the post from this):**
- Feedback that, if followed, would make the post generic ("tone it down," "be less strong," "make it more accessible")
- Contradictory feedback from different reader types (when two agents disagree, default to the founder's natural register)
- Requests to add caveats, disclaimers, or "balance" that would dilute the thesis
- Suggestions to restructure the post around a different angle — that's a different post, not a revision
- Style preferences that don't match the founder's voice (an agent wanting more bullet points when the founder writes in flowing prose)

**CRITICAL RULE: Count the FIX items. If there are more than 3, prioritize the top 3. Trying to fix everything at once is how posts become generic.**

### Step 3: Diagnose before prescribing

For each FIX item, understand WHAT the real problem is before deciding HOW to fix it:

| Feedback | Real problem (usually) | Right fix |
|----------|----------------------|-----------|
| "The opening is weak" | Lacks specificity or open loop | Rewrite first 1-2 lines to be more specific and create curiosity. Keep the same topic/angle. |
| "Not enough evidence" | Assertion without proof | Add 1-2 sentences of concrete detail at the weak point — a number, a quote, a specific moment. |
| "It's too long" | Not that it's long — it's uneven. There's a weak section. | Cut the weakest paragraph entirely rather than trimming every paragraph by 20%. |
| "Doesn't feel authentic" | Vocabulary mismatch | Swap 3-5 generic phrases for the founder's signature language. Add one moment of unexpected specificity. |
| "The ending is flat" | Ends with a summary or generic CTA | Rewrite last 1-2 sentences. Close with a reframe, a provocative question, or a sharp one-liner. |
| "The argument has a gap" | Missing connection between claim and proof | Add 1-2 bridging sentences, not a new section. The post should flow, not lecture. |
| "Too aggressive / too mild" | Emotional register doesn't match the content | Adjust specific word choices and sentence lengths. More aggressive = shorter sentences, harder verbs. More measured = longer sentences, qualifiers that earn precision not weakness. |
| "No clear takeaway" | The turn isn't sharp enough | Rewrite the turn (Layer 3) to be more specific and surprising. Don't add a takeaway section — embed it in the argument. |

### Step 4: Make surgical edits

**The 15-30% rule**: A good revision changes 15-30% of the post. More than that means you're rewriting, not revising. Less means you're not actually addressing the feedback.

For each edit, verify:
- Does this address a specific FIX item?
- Does it preserve the voice? (Read the sentence out loud in the founder's voice)
- Does it improve the post for the majority of reader types, not just the one who complained?
- Does it maintain or increase the post's specificity? (Revisions that remove specific details in favor of general statements are almost always wrong)

**Types of surgical edits (in order of preference):**

1. **Word swaps**: Replace a generic word with a specific one. "Our growth was significant" → "We grew 3x in 11 weeks." Highest impact, lowest disruption.

2. **Sentence rewrites**: Take a weak sentence and make it land harder. Keep the same idea, sharpen the execution.

3. **Targeted additions**: Add 1-2 sentences where the argument has a gap. Don't add a new section — weave it into the existing structure.

4. **Targeted cuts**: Remove the weakest 2-3 sentences. A shorter, tighter post almost always outperforms a longer, "complete" one.

5. **Structural reorder**: Move a paragraph if the flow is wrong. This is rare and should only happen if multiple agents flagged pacing issues.

### Step 5: Anti-genericization check

After revising, scan the new version for signs that you've over-polished:

- [ ] Does the revised post still sound like the SAME PERSON who wrote the original? (If it sounds "more professional," you've gone too far.)
- [ ] Does it still have at least one rough edge, one moment of personality that a writing coach might smooth out? (Real writing has texture.)
- [ ] Have you ADDED any generic phrases that weren't in the original? ("At the end of the day," "The key takeaway," "In conclusion" — these never belong.)
- [ ] Is the revised post still something that could NOT have been written by anyone else in the same industry?
- [ ] Is the thesis still sharp and arguable, or have you added so many caveats that it's now safe and boring?
- [ ] Would the founder read this revision and think "yes, this is me, but better" — not "this doesn't sound like me anymore"?

If any of these checks fail, pull back the relevant edits.

### Step 6: The A/B read

Do one final read of the original, then one final read of the revision. Ask:
- Is the revision clearly better? (If you're not sure, the revision hasn't done enough.)
- Did the revision keep everything that was already working? (If something strong in the original is now missing or weakened, restore it.)
- Is the difference between the two versions something the FOUNDER would notice and appreciate? (If only a writing professor would notice the difference, the revision was too subtle to matter.)

## COMMON PITFALLS TO AVOID

**The hedge creep**: Adding qualifiers to every strong statement because one agent said "too aggressive." Result: a post that says nothing confidently. If the founder's voice is bold, keep it bold.

**The clarity trap**: Making every sentence crystal clear by removing all ambiguity. Result: a boring post. Some tension and mystery is what keeps people reading.

**The length panic**: Cutting the post dramatically because one agent said "too long." Result: losing the proof section that gives the post its credibility. Cut the weakest part, not every part.

**The frankenstein**: Implementing every piece of feedback independently without considering how they interact. Result: a post that's "improved" in 5 ways but doesn't cohere as a single piece of writing.

**The de-risking**: Softening the thesis, adding "of course, it depends," qualifying the claim until it's unfalsifiable. Result: a post nobody disagrees with and nobody remembers.

## ANTI-AI-SLOP CHECK

The revision must NOT introduce any of these patterns that weren't in the original:
- "In today's [anything]" / "In the world of [anything]"
- "landscape" / "navigate" / "leverage" / "foster" / "harness" / "unlock" / "elevate"
- "game-changer" / "deep dive" / "at scale" / "double down"
- "Let that sink in" / "Read that again"
- "It's not about X, it's about Y" (unless the original used this structure)
- "The truth is..." / "Here's the thing..."
- Perfect parallel structure that wasn't in the original (real human revision stays imperfect)

If your revision is smoother and more polished than the original, you've made it sound MORE like AI, not less. Pull back.

- If the feedback is contradictory or would require a completely different post, prioritize the changes that address the most commonly flagged issues while preserving voice fidelity.

- If the original post has fundamental problems (most scores below 5), the revision should be more aggressive — but still preserve the core angle and voice.
