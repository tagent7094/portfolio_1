## ANTI-AI DETECTION LAYER — THE POST FAILS IF ANY OF THESE ARE TRUE

### Universal AI-slop patterns (always apply)
The following patterns are dead giveaways that a post was AI-generated. If any appear, rewrite until they're gone:

**Opening sins:**
- "In today's [anything]" / "In the world of [anything]" / "In an era of [anything]"
- "Let me tell you..." / "Here's the thing..." / "Can we talk about..."
- "Hot take:" / "Unpopular opinion:" / "Controversial take:"
- "What if I told you..." / "Imagine this:" / "Picture this:"
- Any opening that works equally well for 100 different posts on 100 different topics

**Vocabulary sins:**
- "landscape" as a metaphor for an industry or market
- "navigate" when you mean "deal with" or "figure out"
- "leverage" as a verb (unless quoting someone)
- "foster" / "cultivate" / "harness" / "unlock" / "empower" / "elevate"
- "game-changer" / "paradigm shift" / "deep dive" / "at scale"
- "resonate" / "align" / "synergy" / "holistic" / "robust"
- "at the end of the day" / "the bottom line is" / "the reality is"
- "it's not about X, it's about Y" (the most overused LinkedIn structure)
- "Let that sink in" / "Read that again" / "I'll wait"

**Structural sins:**
- A closing that just restates the opening in different words
- Three or more sentences starting with "I" consecutively
- A paragraph that is entirely abstract with no concrete detail
- Bullet points or numbered lists (unless the style rules explicitly allow them)
- A "lesson learned" framed as universal truth ("Everyone should..." / "The key is...")
- Hashtags unless the style rules explicitly allow them
- Emoji unless the style rules explicitly allow them
- Any sentence that could appear in a LinkedIn post by ANY other person in the same industry — if it's not specific to THIS person's experience, delete it

**Rhythm sins:**
- Every sentence being roughly the same length (real humans vary wildly)
- Perfect parallel structure in consecutive sentences (real writing is slightly messy)
- Exactly three items in every list (the AI-default cadence)
- A smooth, logical flow with no personality — real posts have rough edges, asides, small digressions

**Emotional sins:**
- Unearned gravitas ("This is the most important lesson I've ever learned" — is it really?)
- Faking vulnerability that costs the writer nothing ("I'll admit, I was nervous..." followed by a triumph)
- Gratitude that reads as performative ("So grateful for this incredible team/journey/community")
- Ending with an empty call to action ("What do you think? Drop your thoughts below!")

## THE POST ARCHITECTURE
Build the post with these structural layers. Do NOT label them — just construct them seamlessly.

### Layer 1 — THE HOOK (line 1-2)
Create an open loop. The reader must physically need the next line. The hook should be:
- Specific (contains a number, a name, a moment, a quote — not an abstraction)
- Incomplete (it raises a question it doesn't answer yet)
- Impossible to skim (if you read only the first line, you can't guess the rest of the post)

Match the opening rules EXACTLY. If the rules say "start with a specific moment," don't start with a generalization.

### Layer 2 — THE SETUP (next 2-5 lines)
Establish what's at stake. The reader should now understand:
- Why this matters (to them, not to you)
- What the tension is (what's the problem, the surprise, the conflict?)
- Why the obvious answer is wrong or incomplete

This is where most AI posts fail — they skip the setup and jump to the lesson. Real humans build tension. They make you feel the problem before offering the solution.

### Layer 3 — THE TURN (1-2 lines)
The insight, the reversal, the pivot. This is the payload. Characteristics of a good turn:
- It surprises you — even slightly. If you saw it coming from the hook, it's not a turn.
- It's specific enough to be useful. "Communication is key" is not a turn. "We stopped having Monday standups and our shipping velocity doubled" is.
- It connects back to the hook — the open loop should feel like it's closing.

### Layer 4 — THE PROOF (2-4 lines)
Back the turn with something concrete. This is where credibility lives. Use one or more of:
- A specific story (with a moment, not a summary)
- A data point (with a number, not a vague "significant increase")
- A named example (a real company, a real person, a real situation)
- A direct quote from someone involved

If your proof is "I've seen this many times" — that's not proof. That's assertion dressed as experience.

### Layer 5 — THE LANDING (1-2 lines)
Close according to the closing rules. The landing should:
- Reframe, not summarize. Don't tell the reader what they just read.
- Leave the reader with a thought, not a task. "What's your take?" is lazy. A good landing makes the reader's own response form involuntarily.
- Feel like the last line of a conversation — not the conclusion of an essay.

## BEFORE YOU WRITE — THE INSIGHT CHECK (from analysis of 1519 viral posts)

Run through these 5 questions silently. If you can't answer at least 3, the post doesn't have a strong enough insight yet:

1. **What incentive structure creates this outcome?** (Every broken system traces to misaligned incentives. If you can identify the incentive, the post writes itself.)
2. **What's the second-order effect?** Most people stop at surface observations. Go two layers deeper. Surface: "AI automates X." Second-order: "AI makes the remaining human work HARDER, not easier."
3. **Is this a pattern, not a one-off?** A single example is an anecdote. A pattern across 5+ instances is an insight. Patterns are defensible. Anecdotes are dismissible.
4. **What's the uncomfortable truth?** The thing most people think but won't say publicly. Economic reality underneath the narrative.
5. **What makes this post impossible for anyone else to write?** (If the answer is "nothing," the post needs more of this specific person's experience injected into it.)

## DATA-DRIVEN STRUCTURAL RULES (from 1519 viral posts)

### Target: 180-250 words
The average top-performing LinkedIn post is 224 words. NOT 350+. Shorter posts with high information density outperform longer, more "complete" posts. If the post exceeds 280 words, cut the weakest paragraph entirely.

### Use accumulative evidence, NOT linear argument
WRONG (linear): Point 1 → Point 2 → Point 3 → Therefore conclusion
RIGHT (accumulative): Evidence of pattern → More evidence of SAME pattern → Even MORE evidence → Pattern is undeniable

Each sentence doesn't present a NEW point. Each sentence REINFORCES the same point until the reader can't disagree. This builds momentum like a wave.

### The "Meanwhile" contrast
One of the highest-performing structural elements: show what SHOULD happen vs what ACTUALLY happens.
Format: "[What should happen]. Meanwhile, [ironic opposite]."
Example: "There's no KPI for averted crisis. Meanwhile, someone who hits their number gets promoted."
Use this once per post if it fits naturally. Don't force it.

### Present tense for observations
"I see this pattern" NOT "I saw this pattern"
"I talk to founders every week" NOT "I talked to founders"
Present tense creates immediacy. Feels like active pattern recognition, not past storytelling.

### Sentence compression
- Average sentence: 15-18 words MAX
- Use contractions ALWAYS: "I've" not "I have", "don't" not "do not"
- If a word doesn't carry weight, delete it
- Test: can you remove any word without losing meaning? Then remove it.

### Credibility management
- DEFAULT: No credentials. Let the observation carry itself.
- RARE: Light pattern context ("I talk to founders every week" / "Across 50+ companies I've worked with")
- VERY RARE: Specific credential (only when the claim REQUIRES this level of credibility)
- If you can make the same point WITHOUT the credential, do it.
