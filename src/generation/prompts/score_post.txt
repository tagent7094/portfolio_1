## SCORING DIMENSIONS (each 0-10)

### 1. VOICE FIDELITY — Does this sound like the person?

This is the make-or-break dimension for ghostwritten content. A post with a great argument but wrong voice actively HURTS the founder's brand — it signals inauthenticity to anyone who knows them.

- **9-10**: Indistinguishable from their own writing. A close colleague would bet money this person wrote it. The vocabulary, sentence rhythm, emotional register, opening style, and opinion strength ALL match. The post has the founder's specific quirks — not just their "professional voice" but their actual voice.
- **7-8**: Clearly in their voice. A few sentences might feel slightly polished or generic, but the overall impression is authentic. 80%+ of the post sounds like them.
- **5-6**: Recognizable but inconsistent. Some sections sound like them; others could be anyone. A perceptive reader would suspect a ghostwriter. The ideas are right, but the expression isn't quite theirs.
- **3-4**: Generic with their ideas sprinkled in. The opinions are correct, but the writing could have come from any competent LinkedIn poster. The post reads like a brief was given to a writer who never met the founder.
- **0-2**: Doesn't sound like them at all. Wrong vocabulary, wrong rhythm, wrong tone, wrong level of formality. Would confuse their audience.

**Specific signals to check:**
- Opening style: Does it match how they naturally start posts?
- Sentence length variation: Do they write in staccato bursts or flowing paragraphs?
- Pronoun usage: "I" heavy? "We" heavy? Third-person references?
- Emotional temperature: Measured? Passionate? Dry? The register should match.
- Closing pattern: How do they end things? Punchline? Question? Reframe? Trail off?
- Vocabulary: Are there words in this post that this person would NEVER use? (This is often the giveaway.)

### 2. HOOK STRENGTH — Would you stop scrolling?

Score the first 1-3 lines independently. Everything else is irrelevant if the hook fails — because no one sees the rest.

- **9-10**: Irresistible. Creates an open loop that DEMANDS resolution. You physically cannot scroll past without reading the next line. Contains a specific detail that makes it feel real. You re-read the hook because it surprised you.
- **7-8**: Strong pull. An interesting claim, a surprising stat, or a bold statement that earns attention. You'd stop and read the next few lines to see where it's going.
- **5-6**: Decent but expected. It's relevant and clear, but it doesn't create urgency or curiosity. You'd read it if you were already in "reading mode" but wouldn't stop active scrolling for it.
- **3-4**: Weak. It's a topic statement, not a hook. "I've been thinking about X" / "Something interesting happened" / "[Industry] is changing" energy. The reader has no reason to invest.
- **0-2**: Invisible. Indistinguishable from the thousand other posts in the feed. No specificity, no surprise, no tension.

**Hook diagnostic checklist:**
- Does the first line contain a specific detail? (number, name, quote, moment)
- Does it create an open loop? (a question the reader needs answered)
- Does it work on its own? (if you only read line 1, would you need line 2?)
- Is it distinct? (could this hook start a DIFFERENT post on a different topic? If yes, it's too generic)

### 3. ARGUMENT QUALITY — Is the logic sound and the proof real?

- **9-10**: Airtight. The post makes a clear, specific claim → supports it with concrete evidence (not assertion) → and addresses the strongest counterargument either explicitly or by implication. The reader finishes thinking "I can't easily argue with this."
- **7-8**: Strong. The argument is clear and well-supported. Minor gaps a generous reader would overlook. The proof section has at least one concrete detail (a number, a story, a quote).
- **5-6**: Adequate. The point is valid but the support is thin — relies more on assertion than evidence. "I've seen this many times" without the specific time. A skeptical reader would push back.
- **3-4**: Shaky. The post confuses correlation with causation, generalizes from one example to a universal rule, or makes a claim it doesn't bother supporting. The classic "trust me" post.
- **0-2**: Flawed. The logic is broken, the evidence contradicts the claim, or the post argues with a strawman. Would actively damage credibility.

**Argument diagnostic checklist:**
- Can you state the post's main claim in one sentence? (If no, the thesis is unclear)
- Is the proof section based on specific experience or generic assertion?
- Does the post acknowledge any complexity, or does it pretend the world is simple?
- Could a smart, good-faith critic find an obvious hole? (If yes, the argument has a gap)

### 4. ENGAGEMENT PREDICTION — Will people share this?

Score the structural mechanics of engagement — not just "is this good?" but "does the format create sharing and commenting behavior?"

- **9-10**: Highly shareable. Multiple triggers: a quotable line people will screenshot, an emotional response (recognition, outrage, relief, "finally someone said it"), AND the sharer gets social capital for posting it. People will tag specific colleagues.
- **7-8**: Will generate comments and some shares. People will want to add their perspective or push back. The post creates a conversation, not just a reaction.
- **5-6**: Will get steady likes from existing followers. Low share and comment velocity. The post is good but doesn't create the urge to RESPOND.
- **3-4**: Low engagement. Nothing wrong with it, but nothing propels action. Will quietly collect 20-40 likes and disappear. The "nice post" response.
- **0-2**: Will be scrolled past. No emotional hook, no quotable moment, no reason to engage. Dead on arrival.

**Engagement diagnostic checklist:**
- Is there a single line someone would screenshot and share? (The "quotable moment")
- Does the post trigger a "this happened to me too" response? (Drives comments)
- Does it create sides? (Healthy disagreement drives visibility)
- Does sharing this post make the SHARER look good? (The real engine of virality)
- Is there a natural "tag someone who..." impulse? (Not stated — felt)

### 5. AI-SLOP DETECTION — Does this sound like a human or an algorithm?

This is the new dimension and it's arguably the most important for the pipeline. A post can score well on voice, argument, and engagement but STILL feel AI-generated if it uses certain patterns.

- **9-10**: Unmistakably human. Contains imperfections, personality quirks, specific details that no template could produce, and at least one moment that feels slightly raw or unpolished. The kind of writing that makes you think "a real person sat down and wrote this."
- **7-8**: Mostly human. One or two phrases might feel slightly templated, but the overall impression is of genuine human thought. The specificity saves it.
- **5-6**: Ambiguous. A reader might wonder "did AI help with this?" Some sections feel authentic; others feel too smooth, too balanced, or too perfectly structured. The telltale sign: everything is "correct" but nothing is surprising.
- **3-4**: Likely AI-assisted. Vocabulary is too clean, structure is too predictable, opinions are stated in the "on one hand / on the other" format that AI defaults to. Lacks the rough edges of human writing.
- **0-2**: Obviously AI-generated. Uses multiple slop patterns: "In today's [X]," "Let that sink in," "game-changer," perfect parallel structure, no specific details, reads like it was prompted with "write a LinkedIn post about [topic]."

**AI-slop detection checklist (flag each):**
- Uses "landscape" / "navigate" / "leverage" / "foster" / "harness" / "unlock" as verbs?
- Opens with a formula ("In today's..." / "Here's the thing..." / "Hot take:")?
- Contains "Let that sink in" / "Read that again" / "I'll wait"?
- Every sentence is roughly the same length? (Real humans vary wildly)
- The structure is perfectly balanced? (Real posts are slightly lopsided)
- Contains no imperfections — no asides, no rough transitions, no personality?
- Could you swap the author's name for anyone else's and the post still works? (Means it's generic)
- The emotions feel performed rather than felt? ("So grateful for..." / "This was a game-changer...")

## FOUNDER INTERNALIZATION & VIRAL MIMICRY (MANDATE CHECK)
Evaluate these heavily. If the post violates these, cap the composite score at 5:
1. **Deep Internalization**: Is the post reliant entirely on the founder's specific context, history, and narrative arc?
2. **Opening Mimicry/Tension**: Does the hook use strong structural tension without feeling generic or fabricated?
3. **Post-Hook Detachment**: After the hook, is the post decoupled from standard templates and built fully from the founder's world?
4. **Concrete Endings**: Does the post end on a specific detail, name, or unsettled thought? (If it summarizes or uses a CTA, it fundamentally violates the rules.)

## COMPOSITE SCORING

The composite is NOT a simple average. Weight it based on what matters most for ghostwritten content:

`composite = (voice_fidelity * 0.25) + (hook_strength * 0.20) + (argument_quality * 0.20) + (engagement_prediction * 0.15) + (ai_slop_detection * 0.20)`

Voice fidelity and AI-slop detection together account for 45% — because the entire point of the pipeline is producing content that sounds authentically human.

## ANTI-INFLATION MANDATE

The average AI-generated LinkedIn post is a 5 composite. Not a 7. Score honestly:
- A 7 composite means "this is a good post that would perform well." That should be rare from the first draft.
- A 6 means "decent, needs revision." This is the expected range for first drafts.
- An 8+ means "publish-ready, minimal revision needed." This should be exceptional.

If you find yourself routinely scoring above 7, you're calibrated too high.
